01 — The Fraud Landscape

Payment gateways are high-value targets.

A payment gateway sits at the intersection of identity, money, and velocity — exactly what fraudsters need. Understanding the attack surface is the first step to defending it.

Payment gateways like PayPal, Razorpay, and Stripe process millions of transactions daily across diverse merchant categories, user profiles, and geographies. This scale creates both the value and the vulnerability: a successful fraud operation doesn't target individuals — it targets patterns in the system that haven't yet been modeled as risks.

Two attack vectors dominate the fraud landscape at the account level:

ATO
Account Takeover
Compromised legitimate accounts
FAC
Fraudulent Account Creation
Synthetic / stolen identities
MF
Mule Fraud
Money laundering via accounts
CNP
Card Not Present
Stolen card data at checkout

This case study focuses on ATO and FAC — the two vectors most directly related to account-level risk analysis, where transaction data analytics plays the most critical role. Both types of fraud leave data signatures — deviations from established behavioral baselines — that can be surfaced through careful SQL analysis, feature engineering, and rule design.

⚠ Core Challenge

The hardest problem in payment fraud is not detection accuracy — it's precision. Every false positive locks out a legitimate customer. At scale, even a 1% false positive rate across 50K daily transactions means 500 real customers incorrectly blocked per day. The goal is surgical detection: maximum fraud catch rate with minimum collateral damage.

02 — Account Takeover

The attacker doesn't need to create an account. They need to steal one.

ATO is the most economically efficient form of payment fraud. Legitimate accounts have established trust, payment methods on file, and cleared KYC — attackers inherit all of it.

How ATO Attacks Are Structured

ATO is rarely a single-step crime. It follows a predictable kill-chain, and each step produces detectable data signals:

Phishing, breach, dark web

The ATO Data Timeline

When an account is taken over, the transaction data tells the story — if you know what to look for:

T − 48 hrs
Abnormal Login Geography
Login attempt from IP in a different country/city than the account's 90-day login history. Device fingerprint is new. User-agent string matches a known credential-stuffing bot framework.
T − 24 hrs
Account Enumeration Pattern
Multiple failed login attempts from the same IP subnet across different accounts — a classic credential stuffing pattern. Eventually one succeeds.
T − 6 hrs
Profile Modification
Password changed. Recovery email/phone updated. These changes suppress victim notifications and extend the attacker's access window.
T − 1 hr
Recon Transactions
Small payments (₹1–₹50) to low-risk merchants. The attacker is "testing" that the account and linked instruments are live and unblocked.
T = 0
Cashout Event
Single large transaction to a newly registered payee — often a high-risk merchant category (gift cards, crypto, money transfer). Amount typically just below the manual review threshold.
T + 2 hrs
Victim Discovery
The legitimate account holder logs in from their known device, sees the transactions, and raises a dispute. By this point, funds are often already moved.

"The attacker's greatest advantage is time. Every hour between takeover and detection is money in transit. The analyst's job is to collapse that window to minutes."

03 — Fraudulent Account Creation

Building fraud from scratch: synthetic identity and account farms.

FAC doesn't rely on stealing an existing account — it manufactures a new one. The challenge is that these accounts look real at creation, and only reveal their fraudulent nature over time.

Types of Fraudulent Account Creation

🧬
Synthetic Identity Fraud

A synthetic identity combines real data elements (a valid PAN or Aadhaar number, often belonging to a minor or low-activity individual) with fabricated data. The resulting "person" passes basic KYC checks but doesn't exist. These accounts are carefully nurtured with small legitimate transactions over months before a final high-value cashout ("bust-out").

🤖
Bot-Farmed Accounts

Automated scripts create hundreds of accounts simultaneously, each using a unique email, device-spoofed fingerprints, and VPN-rotated IPs. Used immediately for referral abuse, bonus farming, or as mule nodes in money laundering networks. Registration velocity and email domain clustering are the primary detection signals.

🪪
Stolen Identity Accounts

Real PII obtained from data breaches is used to create accounts that pass full KYC verification. Unlike synthetic identities, these accounts have a real victim whose credit or financial standing may be damaged. Detection relies on behavioral biometrics, device analysis, and cross-referencing with known breach datasets.

💼
Money Mule Accounts

Recruited (often unknowing) individuals open real accounts and receive/forward funds. These pass KYC because they're real people. Detection requires network analysis — flagging accounts that consistently receive funds from flagged sources and immediately forward them, forming a "layering" pattern.

🔍 Key Insight — The Bust-Out Pattern

Synthetic identity accounts follow a "credit building" arc before defrauding the platform. They make small, legitimate transactions for 30–90 days, build transaction history, and then execute a single large fraudulent cashout. The sudden spike in transaction value relative to historical average is the most reliable detection signal, but it arrives late. Earlier detection requires registration-time signals and network graph analysis.

04 — Detection Methodology

A layered defense: from real-time rules to statistical models.

No single detection method catches all fraud. Effective fraud detection stacks multiple approaches, each optimized for different fraud types and time horizons.

⚡
Layer 1: Real-time Rules

Latency: <100ms. Hard-coded business logic that fires synchronously during transaction authorization. Examples: block transactions from sanctioned IP ranges, block payments to MCC 6051 (crypto) above ₹10,000 from accounts <7 days old. High precision but limited recall — can only catch known patterns.

📊
Layer 2: Statistical Anomaly

Latency: 30s–5min. Rolling window analysis comparing current behavior against the account's own historical baseline. A transaction that is 4σ above the account's 30-day spend average triggers a review flag — regardless of absolute amount. This catches account-specific deviations that rule-based systems miss.

🕸️
Layer 3: Network / Graph Analysis

Latency: Batch (hourly). Maps fund flow between accounts. Identifies "hub-and-spoke" patterns where one account distributes to many receivers (mule coordination), or chains of pass-through transfers (layering). Also clusters new accounts by shared registration attributes to surface bot farms.

🧠
Layer 4: ML Scoring

Latency: 200–500ms. A gradient boosted model scores each transaction on ~150 features derived from transaction data, device signals, and behavioral history. The score is consumed by downstream rule engines. Model is retrained weekly on confirmed fraud labels. This catches novel patterns that no rule yet covers.

05 — Behavioral Signal Analysis

Fraud leaves fingerprints in the data.

Every fraudulent action creates a measurable deviation from expected behavior. The analyst's job is to define "expected" precisely enough that deviations become actionable.

Building the Behavioral Baseline

Effective anomaly detection requires a per-account baseline — not a population average. A merchant processing ₹5L/day isn't anomalous; a personal account that suddenly processes ₹5L/day almost certainly is:

SQL — ClickHouse-- Build per-account behavioral baseline (30-day rolling) WITH baseline AS ( SELECT account_id, count() AS txn_count_30d, avg(amount_paise) AS avg_amount, stddevPop(amount_paise) AS std_amount, quantile(0.95)(amount_paise) AS p95_amount, uniqExact(device_fingerprint) AS unique_devices, uniqExact(login_country) AS unique_countries FROM payments_unified WHERE ts >= now() - INTERVAL 30 DAY AND status = 'SUCCESS' GROUP BY account_id ) SELECT t.account_id, t.txn_id, t.amount_paise, (t.amount_paise - b.avg_amount) / nullIf(b.std_amount, 0) AS z_score, CASE WHEN z_score > 4 THEN 'CRITICAL' WHEN z_score > 3 THEN 'HIGH' WHEN z_score > 2 THEN 'MEDIUM' ELSE 'NORMAL' END AS risk_tier FROM payments_unified t JOIN baseline b USING (account_id) WHERE t.ts >= now() - INTERVAL 5 MINUTE AND z_score > 2;

ATO Detection Query — Triple Conjunction

The most reliable ATO signal is the combination of a new device + profile change + high-value transaction within a 6-hour window. This triple-conjunction pattern has a very low false positive rate:

SQL — ATO Detection-- New device + profile change + high txn within 6 hours WITH new_device_logins AS ( SELECT account_id, device_fingerprint, login_ts, login_country FROM login_events l WHERE login_ts >= now() - INTERVAL 6 HOUR AND NOT EXISTS ( SELECT 1 FROM login_events h WHERE h.account_id = l.account_id AND h.device_fingerprint = l.device_fingerprint AND h.login_ts < l.login_ts - INTERVAL 90 DAY ) ), profile_changes AS ( SELECT account_id, change_type, changed_at FROM audit_log WHERE change_type IN ('PASSWORD_RESET', 'EMAIL_CHANGE', 'PHONE_CHANGE') AND changed_at >= now() - INTERVAL 6 HOUR ), high_value_txns AS ( SELECT t.account_id, t.txn_id, t.amount_paise, t.ts FROM payments_unified t JOIN account_baseline b USING (account_id) WHERE t.ts >= now() - INTERVAL 6 HOUR AND t.amount_paise > b.p95_amount * 3 AND t.status = 'SUCCESS' ) SELECT d.account_id, d.login_country, p.change_type, h.txn_id, h.amount_paise / 100 AS amount_inr, 'ATO_HIGH_CONFIDENCE' AS fraud_signal FROM new_device_logins d JOIN profile_changes p ON d.account_id = p.account_id JOIN high_value_txns h ON d.account_id = h.account_id ORDER BY h.amount_paise DESC;

FAC Bot Farm Cohort Detection

SQL — FAC Bot Farm Detection-- Cluster new accounts by shared registration signals WITH new_accounts AS ( SELECT account_id, substring(registration_ip, 1, findLastOccurrence(registration_ip, '.')) AS ip_subnet, email_domain, device_fingerprint, registration_ts FROM accounts WHERE registration_ts >= now() - INTERVAL 24 HOUR ), cohort_analysis AS ( SELECT ip_subnet, email_domain, count() AS account_count, uniqExact(device_fingerprint) AS unique_devices, dateDiff('second', min(registration_ts), max(registration_ts)) AS time_span_secs, account_count / nullIf(unique_devices, 0) AS accounts_per_device FROM new_accounts GROUP BY ip_subnet, email_domain ) SELECT *, CASE WHEN accounts_per_device > 5 AND time_span_secs < 3600 THEN 'BOT_FARM_HIGH' WHEN account_count > 10 AND unique_devices < account_count / 2 THEN 'BOT_FARM_MEDIUM' ELSE 'NORMAL' END AS bot_farm_signal FROM cohort_analysis WHERE account_count >= 5 ORDER BY accounts_per_device DESC;
06 — Rule Design & Impact Analysis

Rules are hypotheses. Every change has a blast radius.

Writing a fraud rule is easy. Writing one that reduces fraud without blocking legitimate customers requires rigorous impact analysis before every deployment.

RULE-ATO-001 — New Device + High Value
ATOReal-time
Flag for step-up authentication when: (1) login from device not seen in 90 days AND (2) transaction amount > 3× account P95. Action: require OTP before authorizing. Do not hard-block to avoid false positive lockouts.
IF device_is_new(90d) AND amount > account_p95 * 3 THEN action = 'STEP_UP_AUTH', risk_score += 45
RULE-ATO-002 — Profile Change + Transaction Proximity
ATOReal-time
Hard block any transaction above ₹5,000 within 6 hours of a password reset, email change, or phone number change. The combination of credential modification followed by a transaction is a near-certain ATO signal.
IF profile_change_within(6h) AND amount_inr > 5000 THEN action = 'HARD_BLOCK', alert_team = 'RISK_OPS', risk_score += 80
RULE-FAC-001 — New Account High-Risk MCC
FACReal-time
Block payments to MCC categories 6051 (crypto), 6211 (securities), and 7995 (gambling) for accounts less than 7 days old or with fewer than 5 prior transactions. These MCCs are primary cashout vectors for new fraudulent accounts.
IF (account_age_days < 7 OR prior_txn_count < 5) AND mcc IN (6051, 6211, 7995) THEN action = 'HARD_BLOCK', risk_score += 70
RULE-VEL-001 — Transaction Velocity Spike
ATO + FACNear Real-time
Flag accounts with more than 3 transactions in any 10-minute window where the account's typical frequency is less than 2 transactions per hour. Velocity spikes are characteristic of automated cashout scripts.
IF txn_count_last_10min > 3 AND account_typical_hourly_txns < 2 THEN action = 'SOFT_BLOCK', risk_score += 55 -- pause + alert

Blast Radius Analysis Before Deployment

Before any rule goes live, a blast radius analysis measures what percentage of clean transactions would have been incorrectly flagged:

SQL — Blast Radius-- Simulate rule against 30-day historical data before deployment WITH simulation AS ( SELECT t.txn_id, t.merchant_category, t.amount_paise, (profile_change_within_6h AND t.amount_paise > 500000) AS would_block, (t.txn_id IN (SELECT txn_id FROM confirmed_fraud)) AS is_fraud FROM payments_historical_30d t JOIN account_profile_changes a USING (account_id) ) SELECT merchant_category, countIf(would_block) AS total_blocked, countIf(would_block AND is_fraud) AS true_positives, countIf(would_block AND NOT is_fraud) AS false_positives, round(true_positives / total_blocked * 100, 2) AS precision_pct FROM simulation GROUP BY merchant_category ORDER BY false_positives DESC;
07 — Containment & Resolution

Detection is half the battle. Resolution is the other half.

When a fraud case is confirmed, the response must be fast, structured, and minimize both fraud loss and customer impact.

ATO Containment Playbook

Immediate Account Freeze (T+0)
As soon as the ATO signal crosses the HIGH threshold, freeze all outbound transactions. Do not close the account — this preserves the ability to reverse pending transactions. Send an automated notification to the account's verified pre-attack contact.
Transaction Reversal Window (T+0 to T+30min)
For transactions in PENDING or AUTHORIZED state, initiate reversal immediately. For SETTLED transactions, open a chargeback/recall with the receiving institution. Time is critical — once funds are forwarded by the mule, recovery probability drops below 20%.
Payee Account Flagging (T+5min)
The payee that received the fraudulent funds must be flagged and frozen simultaneously. This account is likely a mule. Run it through the network graph to identify other accounts in the same cluster — ATO cashout networks are rarely single-account operations.
Root Cause Classification (T+1hr)
Classify the attack vector: credential stuffing, phishing, social engineering, or insider threat. Pull the full data lineage — from login attempt through transaction — and document the attack path for the case file. This classification feeds back into rule improvement.
Account Recovery (T+24hr)
Restore access to the legitimate account holder via out-of-band verification. Reset all credentials. Review and remove any profile changes made during the attack window. Issue a formal incident notification to the customer.
Rule Refinement (T+48hr)
If this attack bypassed existing rules, add it to the false-negative log. Analyze what signal was present that no rule was catching. Design a new rule, run blast radius analysis, and deploy. This feedback loop is what makes detection continuously improve.
📋 Bot Farm Response

When a bot farm cohort is identified: (1) Batch-suspend all accounts in the cluster simultaneously — staggered suspension allows attackers to detect the pattern and adapt. (2) Flag all transactions from cluster accounts for reversal review. (3) Block the IP subnet and device fingerprint classes at the WAF level. (4) If referral codes were abused, invalidate the associated rewards in bulk.

🧬 Synthetic Identity Response

Synthetic identities require a different approach: (1) Do not close the account immediately — this tips off the fraudster network. Instead, silently apply enhanced monitoring and transaction limits. (2) Submit identity documents to the KYC provider for re-verification. (3) If confirmed synthetic, file a Suspicious Transaction Report (STR) with the FIU. (4) Use the confirmed label to improve the ML model's precision on future cases.

Key Learnings

1. False positives are not just a customer experience problem — they are an intelligence problem. Every incorrectly blocked legitimate transaction is a signal that the rule is miscalibrated. Tracking false positive rates by segment reveals exactly which rules need adjustment.

2. Fraud detection and fraud investigation must be coupled. Rules fire the alert; the investigation determines the root cause. Without systematic root cause classification, the same attack pattern will recur in slightly modified form. Every investigated case should generate at least one rule improvement hypothesis.

3. Attackers adapt to detection. Static rules decay rapidly. A fraud rule that achieved 85% precision at launch will typically degrade to 60% within 90 days as attackers learn to avoid its trigger conditions. Continuous rule review and model retraining are the operating rhythm of effective fraud risk management.

4. The blast radius framework must be non-negotiable. Every rule change, however small, must be simulated against historical data before deployment. This is the difference between deploying a rule that blocks 500 legitimate customers per day and one that doesn't.

✅ Core Principle

The best fraud analysts don't just investigate individual incidents — they model the system that produces fraud. Every case is a data point. Every rule is a hypothesis. Every outcome is a label. The goal is a continuously improving signal-to-noise ratio across the entire transaction portfolio.
